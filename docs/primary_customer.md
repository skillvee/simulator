## The Primary Buyer: Tech Hiring Managers

### Who They Are

Tech Hiring Managers are the people responsible for building and growing engineering teams. They typically hold titles like:

- Engineering Manager
- Senior Engineering Manager
- Director of Engineering
- VP of Engineering
- Head of Engineering
- Principal/Staff Engineer (with hiring responsibilities)

They are **not recruiters**. While recruiters manage candidate pipelines and process, hiring managers:
- Define what "good" looks like for their team
- Make final hiring decisions
- Onboard new hires onto their team
- Live with the consequences when a hire doesn't work out

### What They Care About Most

#### 1. Team Productivity & Velocity
Engineering managers are measured on what their team ships. Every hiring decision directly impacts their ability to deliver. They ask themselves:
- "Will this person make my team faster or slower?"
- "How long until they're productive?"
- "Will I need to spend significant time mentoring them?"

> 65% of engineering leaders reject quantity-based metrics like lines of code, favoring quality-focused measures that better reflect real impact. ([LeadDev](https://leaddev.com/the-engineering-team-performance-report-2025))

#### 2. Team Dynamics & Culture Fit
A bad hire doesn't just fail to contribute—they actively harm the team:
- Demotivate high performers
- Create tension and conflict
- Cause top talent to leave

> "One of the most overlooked consequences of a bad hire is the effect on existing team members. A poor performer or disruptive personality can demotivate skilled workers, foster resentment, or even cause high-performing employees to leave." ([HBK](https://hbkcpa.com/insights/the-hidden-costs-of-bad-hiring-how-to-calculate-your-true-cost-per-hire/))

#### 3. Code Quality & Technical Standards
They want engineers who:
- Write clean, maintainable code
- Understand testing and quality practices
- Can work independently without constant review
- Don't introduce technical debt

> Engineering leaders are balancing rapid feature delivery with sustainable codebase health. Investing in long-term stability means fewer emergencies and smoother development. ([LeadDev](https://leaddev.com/career-development/what-engineering-managers-need-to-know-for-2025))

#### 4. Communication & Collaboration Skills
Modern engineering is collaborative. They need people who:
- Can explain their work clearly
- Participate effectively in code reviews
- Communicate proactively about blockers
- Work well with product, design, and other stakeholders

> "In today's world, a hiring manager is just as likely to be looking for teamwork and general problem-solving skills as they are hard technical skills." ([Built In](https://builtin.com/software-engineering-perspectives/what-look-when-hiring-software-engineers))

#### 5. Growth Mindset & Learning Ability
Technology changes constantly. They value:
- Curiosity and willingness to learn
- Ability to adapt to new tools and frameworks
- Coachability and openness to feedback
- Self-directed professional development

> "It's not about whether they know all the answers on day one—we look for curious people who are lifelong learners with a drive to improve themselves." ([Formation.dev](https://formation.dev/blog/how-to-pass-hiring-manager-interviews/))

## Their Pain Points

### Pain Point #1: Traditional Interviews Don't Predict Success

The current interview process is broken:

- **Whiteboard/LeetCode tests measure the wrong things**: Algorithmic puzzles test memorization, not real-world ability
- **Interviews create artificial pressure**: High-stress environments don't reflect actual work conditions
- **Resumes and credentials are unreliable**: Credentials don't predict job performance

> "Unstructured interviews predict job success only about 14% of the time, and 74% of employers admit to making a bad hire because of relying too heavily on resumes or gut instinct." ([Canditech](https://www.canditech.io/the-complete-guide-for-job-simulations-in-the-candidate-screening-process/))

> "Many interviews feel like an outdated approach—too fixated on algorithmic trickery rather than the practical, collaborative skills that truly drive the industry forward." ([Greenhouse](https://www.greenhouse.com/blog/why-engineers-refuse-coding-tests-for-interviews))

**What they really want**: A way to see how candidates actually work—their process, communication, problem-solving approach, and collaboration style.

### Pain Point #2: The Devastating Cost of Bad Hires

Bad hires are catastrophically expensive:

- **Direct costs**: 30-150% of annual salary to replace
- **Manager time**: 17% of manager time supervising poor performers (almost a full day per week)
- **Team impact**: Overtime for others covering gaps, damaged morale, potential attrition of good people
- **Project delays**: Missed deadlines, quality issues, rework

> "A bad hire in a technical field costs up to 150% of their annual salary to replace... 1 in 4 companies lost over $50,000 in this process." ([Apollo Technical](https://www.apollotechnical.com/cost-of-a-bad-hire/))

> "34% of CFOs said that not only do bad hires cost them productivity, but managers also have to spend 17% of their time supervising poorly-performing employees." ([Apollo Technical](https://www.apollotechnical.com/cost-of-a-bad-hire/))

**Real example**: A mid-sized engineering firm hired a project manager lacking compliance knowledge. Result: two delayed projects worth $1.2M in revenue, six weeks of manager time on remediation, and overtime costs for team members covering the gap. Total cost exceeded $150,000. ([Metalis](https://www.metalis.co.uk/blog/2025/07/the-hidden-cost-of-a-bad-hire))

### Pain Point #3: Can't Assess "How" They Work

Current tools only measure **what** candidates produce, not **how** they work:

- Do they use AI tools effectively?
- How do they communicate when stuck?
- Do they test their code?
- How do they handle feedback and code review?
- Do they manage their time well?
- How do they collaborate with stakeholders?

> "Employers currently have higher expectations of AI literacy among job candidates than in the past. It's not just about whether a job applicant can code, but how they are integrating AI into their work." ([GoodTime](https://goodtime.io/blog/recruiting/tech-hiring-trends/))

### Pain Point #4: Time-to-Hire Is Too Long

Extended hiring processes hurt everyone:

- 69% of tech companies reported increased time-to-hire in 2024
- Average tech hiring takes 52+ days (vs. 44 days across industries)
- AI/ML roles take 89 days on average to fill
- Senior candidates get multiple offers and accept elsewhere

> "Bottlenecks such as untrained or underprepared interviewers (42%) and poor communication with candidates (42%) were among the leading factors contributing to delays." ([GoodTime](https://goodtime.io/blog/recruiting/tech-hiring-trends/))

**The challenge**: They need to make faster decisions without sacrificing quality.

### Pain Point #5: Senior Candidates Resist Traditional Assessments

Top senior engineers refuse to:
- Spend hours on take-home projects
- Do whiteboard algorithm problems
- "Prove themselves" with tests that don't reflect real work

> "Senior developers are sometimes unwilling to complete coding assessments simply because they feel like they shouldn't need to prove themselves—they've been working for years and have countless products and code samples they can instead highlight." ([Qualified.io](https://www.qualified.io/blog/posts/why-developers-hate-coding-skills-tests))

**The paradox**: The best candidates are the hardest to assess with traditional methods.

### Pain Point #6: Volume of Applicants

Application volume has exploded:
- One Y Combinator startup received 23,000 applications in 30 days for 8 roles
- Spotify received 1,700 applicants in 15 hours for one role
- Companies report going from 1-5 resumes/week to 300+/week

> "The biggest trend hiring managers and recruiters report is how much bigger the volume of applications is." ([Pragmatic Engineer](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/state-of-the-tech-market-in-2025-hiring-managers))

**The result**: Signal-to-noise ratio is terrible. Great candidates get lost in the flood.

## What They Want in a Solution

### Must-Have Qualities

1. **Predictive validity**: Actually correlates with job performance
2. **Shows real work behavior**: Not just outputs, but process and communication
3. **Respects candidate time**: Not a multi-hour burden
4. **Works for senior candidates**: Doesn't feel demeaning or irrelevant
5. **Provides actionable signal**: Clear, defensible data for decisions
6. **Integrates into existing process**: Doesn't require overhauling their workflow

### Why Work Sample Tests Work

Research strongly supports work sample tests and job simulations:

> "Work sample tests demonstrate high validity because tasks applicants are asked to perform are very representative of the tasks performed on the job." ([OPM.gov](https://www.opm.gov/policy-data-oversight/assessment-and-selection/other-assessment-methods/work-samples-and-simulations/))

> "82% of companies that use work sample assessments report improved quality of hires, and 89% of businesses report increased employee retention after implementing skills-based hiring practices." ([Canditech](https://www.canditech.io/the-complete-guide-for-job-simulations-in-the-candidate-screening-process/))

> "Harvard Business Review emphasizes that work sample tests, which simulate the tasks the candidate will undertake in the role, are paramount indicators of future job performance." ([Vervoe](https://vervoe.com/job-simulations/))

**Skillvee's differentiation**: We don't just test code output—we simulate an entire day at work to reveal communication, collaboration, AI usage, time management, and problem-solving process.

## Secondary Priority: Product Management Hiring Managers

### Why PM Hiring Is Also a Target

Product management hiring faces similar challenges:
- Role is broad and not standardized across companies
- Hard to assess "product sense" in interviews
- Success depends on how they work, not just what they know
- CVs are of limited value in assessing PM skills

> "Hiring product managers remains hard because the role is broad but not standardized – product is 'done' differently everywhere – and it's hard to assess someone's product sense at an interview." ([Mind the Product](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/why-it-is-so-hard-to-hire-product-managers/))

### What PM Hiring Managers Look For

1. **Problem-solving ability**: "I'll take a wickedly smart, inexperienced PM over one of average intellect and years of experience any day." ([Ken Norton](https://www.bringthedonuts.com/essays/productmanager.html))

2. **Customer focus & curiosity**: Are they customer-first? Problem-first? Curious and collaborative?

3. **Ownership & resilience**: Ability to drive through ambiguity and overcome obstacles

4. **Communication & influence**: Can they build chemistry and generate enthusiasm across teams?

5. **Technical & business acumen**: Can they work effectively with engineering and understand constraints?

> "In a survey by Pragmatic Institute, 81% of respondents cited 'balancing technical and soft skills' as a top hiring challenge for PMs." ([ProductPlan](https://www.productplan.com/learn/hire-product-manager/))

**The good news**: Series B-D startups have strong employer brands. They've proven product-market fit, have funding, offer equity upside, and work on interesting problems. Top talent wants to work there.

**The challenge**: How do you maintain hiring quality when you need to hire 5-10+ engineers per month while processing hundreds of applications per role?

## Summary: The Core Insight

Tech Hiring Managers at high-growth startups face a unique challenge: they have strong employer brands that attract floods of applicants, aggressive hiring targets that demand speed, and small teams where every hire matters enormously. They've been burned by bad hires that looked great on paper. They know interviews are broken but don't have better options.

**What they need**: A way to see candidates working like they would on the job—communicating with stakeholders, using AI tools, managing their time, handling feedback, and solving real problems. For a startup hiring 5+ engineers per month, this means shipping faster, preserving culture, and avoiding the $150K+ cost of each bad hire.
