## 1. HN: "AI Broke Interviews"

**Status:** Recent - likely still open for comments

**Response:**
The fundamental problem isn't AI cheating - it's that we've been testing the wrong things for years. AI just made it obvious.

LeetCode tests recall. Take-homes test free time. Neither tests how someone actually works: how they handle ambiguous requirements, communicate with stakeholders, ask clarifying questions, or make tradeoffs under time pressure.

I've been working on this problem. Instead of trying to make interviews "AI-proof," we flipped the question: what if you simulate an actual day at work? You're not testing what they know, you're watching how they think and collaborate.

Early version at skillvee.com if anyone wants to try it. But honestly the bigger insight is just: stop testing things AI can do better than humans, and start testing what makes humans valuable.

## 3. HN: "Job-seekers are dodging AI interviewers"
**URL:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=44783155
**Status:** Aug 2025 - may be locked

**Response:**
There's a crucial difference between "AI replacing the interviewer" and "AI creating a realistic scenario for the candidate." The first is dehumanizing. The second can actually be better than a human interview.

Think about it - in a traditional interview, your experience depends entirely on who you get. Bad interviewer? Bad day for the interviewer? You're toast. An AI-simulated work scenario can be consistent, fair, and realistic without the power dynamics that make interviews feel adversarial.

We're building something along these lines (skillvee.com) - the AI doesn't interview you, it plays the role of a manager giving you a realistic task, a coworker you collaborate with. The candidate does real work, not perform for a judge. The difference in candidate experience is night and day.

The backlash against AI interviewers makes sense because those tools are automating the wrong part. The interview itself is the problem - automate the scenario, not the judgment.

## 4. Dev.to: "Adapting Technical Interviews to Counter AI-Assisted Cheating"
**URL:** https://dev.to/jotafeldmann/adapting-technical-interviews-to-counter-ai-assisted-cheating-36lk
**Status:** Evergreen - comments always open on Dev.to

**Response:**
Great breakdown. The point about contextual exercises from your actual codebase is key - but I'd push it further. Even personalized coding exercises still mostly test "can this person write code," which AI has made table-stakes.

The dimension that's hardest to fake - and most predictive of on-the-job success - is how someone navigates ambiguity. Do they ask clarifying questions or just start coding? When requirements conflict, do they flag it or make assumptions silently? How do they communicate tradeoffs?

We've been building SkillVee around this idea - instead of a coding test, it's a simulated workday. Candidate talks to an AI "manager" who gives them an intentionally ambiguous brief, they work through the task, then defend their choices in a code review. The AI evaluates the whole process - communication, problem decomposition, judgment - not just whether the code compiles.

89% of bad hires fail on soft skills, not technical gaps. Yet we keep optimizing our screening for technical signal. Seems like the wrong optimization target.
